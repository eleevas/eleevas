Scheduling web app

Case Study.

Brief

Human Resources has to manually find a time slot for everyone involved in hiring interviews. Current workflow is tedious and time consuming for HR staff due to back and forth communication.

My role

As a part of a two-men development team, we get to wear many hats. This can be great in terms that I get to employ different skillsets, but also challenging because there's more things to handle at the same time.

I was responsible for creating the initial IA and features, User Interface & Experience, ideating User Journeys, making interactive pages in HTML + CSS, and receiving constructive feedback.

Victor, a brilliant engineer, developed the backend functionality, and added the javascript magic. He was also in charge of managing the project.

Together, we were constantly exchanging ideas for improvements, tested our product, bugfixed, and held meetings with the stakeholders and users to validate usability.

The Problem

The product has to fit the needs of two distinct userbases.

We identified two groups:

  • Users (employees)
    • HR Staff
    • HR Lead
    • Department Team Leads
    • CEO
  • Candidates (job applicants).

The challenge is to provide great UX with two different layouts for all participants in hiring interviews. Since both groups communicate virtually, we have to account for interviews being scheduled in different timezones.

Validating the designs

In separate user interviews with HR Lead and CEO, I tested Figma prototypes to check if we're on the same page regarding the workflow and business requirements.

At this stage, it was decided we will remove the option for the Candidates to cancel the meeting, so I continued without those slides.

After positive feedbacks, and apparently no known issues with the workflow, I used the prototypes as guides for creating a responsive layout page using HTML and CSS from scratch.

Figma - Users and Candidates

All fonts and colors are defined with CSS variables. In case of the need for drastic appeareance change, this can be done with ease.

I also created a dark theme for this design system. To view the screenshots of layouts on the dark side, press the "Jedi" light theme, press the "Empire" button in the middle of the header.

Key learning points

Creating the design system early on:

  • Provides the opportunity to test the interaction of different elements, and fix the issues on the spot.

  • Enables rapid delivery of different layouts, in a form of interactive pages, for user testing.

  • Design system can be re-used in other projects with minimal changes.

Taking into account recommendations from Jakob Nielsen's article, "Why You Only Need to Test with 5 Users", noting that it's possible to learn about 85% of errors, problems, and behavior from the first 5 users. Five users would bring enough data and inputs to uncover issues with our design. It's easier, cheaper and more time / cost efficient to conduct several rounds of testing with a smaller group in qualitative research.

Qualitative Research

Once we had all interactive layouts ready, it was time for qualitative user testing. We surveyed a small group of users in several rounds, and gathered their feedback.

Surveyed audience

The audience consisted of 5 people, a mix of men and women, from 23 to 46 years of age. The survey consisted of several tasks, for example:

"Describe how would you change your timezone."
"Changing the time zone was simple. In the settings window I choose the time zone from the dropdown menu and clicked on Save Profile."
or

"How would you add a new role?"
"By finding the Members windows and scrolling down to Manage Roles I was able to type the new role. Then I clicked on the + button and I successfully aded a new role. Adding a role was simple."

We discovered a pattern on the Availability page.

Setting availability is an expected usecase that Users would do once per week or month. Because 2 out of 5 users were found to be confused, the current workflow needed to be fixed.

"Had confusion with saving availability; first I didn’t click save, second time I didn’t click + to add availability so it didn’t get saved. Perhaps some short instructions would make sure to avoid confusion."
"I found the Availability window and selected the time and then I clicked on the Save button below, but it wasnt saved. So on the second try I realized I need to click on the + button before clicking Save. From there, adding the other availability time was simple. So it might be good to add a little instruction line."

The Problem

How can we avoid users' confusion when setting their availability?

In additional rounds of surveys, we were testing out couple of ideas based on the feedback we received.

"Currently availability can only be deleted, perhaps it would be useful to be able to edit it."
"Currently availability can only be added per day, perhaps it would be useful to be able to add for multiple days same availability"

Based upon the results, we changed form submission to include fields without the need to press the "+" button, we changed the look of this button and we added the ability to "Clone previous week". This is a one click solution for typical usecase when availability doesn't change frequently. If there's a need to add changes, it can easily be done by adjusting time entries.

Key learning points

Qualitative research with small sample of 3-5 users can uncover issues early in the process. It's cost-effective, fast, and easy way to validate design concepts. User survey proved that there was a design bias and we were able to address the issue and re-test.

Conclusion

For the Candidates invited to the interviews, we've provided a way to schedule the interview when they are best prepared.

By optimizing Users workflow, we're saving, on average, 21 hours monthly for employees involved in scheduling interviews.
